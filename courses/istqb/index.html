🔑 Key Distinctions to Remember

Verification vs Validation

  • Verification: "Are we building the product right?"
  • Validation: "Are we building the right product?"

Static vs Dynamic

  • Static: Without code execution → finds defects
  • Dynamic: With code execution → finds failures

Testing vs QA

  • Testing (QC): Product-focused, corrective
  • QA: Process-focused, preventive

Product Risk vs Project Risk

  • Product risk: CAN be affected by testing
  • Project risk: CANNOT be affected by testing

Chapter 1 — Fundamentals of Testing

What is testing · Why is it necessary · 7 principles · Activities · Roles

📖 Official ISTQB Definition

"The process consisting of all life cycle activities, both static and dynamic, concerned with planning, preparation and evaluation of software products and related work products to determine that they satisfy specified requirements, to demonstrate that they are fit for purpose and to detect defects."

— ISTQB Glossary

Verification

Evaluates artifacts (requirements, design, code) to ensure they meet specified requirements and standards.
"Making the product right."

Validation

Evaluates software to meet the user's needs and requirements.
"Making the right product."

⚖️ The 7 Testing Principles

1
Testing shows the presence of defects, not their absence Testing reduces the probability of remaining defects, but can never prove the software is defect-free.
2
Exhaustive testing is impossible Use test techniques, prioritization, and risk-based testing instead of trying to test all combinations. (20 windows × 4 menus × 3 sub-options × 10 fields × 100 choices = 240,000 combinations!)
3
Early testing saves time and money The earlier defects and failures are discovered, the simpler and cheaper they are to fix. Testing activities should start as early as possible in the SDLC.
4
Defects cluster together (Pareto principle) A small number of components usually contain most of the defects. 20% of modules contain 80% of defects → basis for risk-based testing.
5
Tests wear out (Pesticide Paradox) When a test is repeated over and over, it eventually stops finding new defects. Review and modify test cases regularly. Exception: confirmation testing and regression testing.
6
Testing is context dependent Safety-critical software is tested more formally than a webshop. Context factors: risks, time, money, test levels, SDLC model, organizational domain.
7
Absence-of-defects fallacy Finding and fixing defects does not help if the system does not fulfill the user's needs and expectations. A slightly flawed but usable system may be better than a defect-free but unusable one.
Exam tip: All 7 principles are exam favorites. Especially principle 1 (cannot prove absence), 2 (exhaustive testing impossible), and 7 (absence-of-defects fallacy).

🤝 Whole Team Approach & Independence

Whole Team Approach

  • The entire team (testers, developers, business) shares quality responsibility
  • 3–9 people, all necessary skills, daily stand-ups
  • Limitation: Not sufficient when high independence is required (e.g. safety-critical)

Independence Levels (weakest → strongest)

  • No independent testers (developer tests own code)
  • Independent testers within the development team
  • Independent testers within the organization
  • Testers from the business/user community
  • Testers external to the organization

Chapter 2 — Testing in the SDLC

5 test levels · 4 test types · SDLC models · Shift-left · TDD/BDD

🏗️ SDLC Models and Testing

Sequential models (Waterfall, V-model)

Static testing early; dynamic testing starts late. Late changes are challenging. Testing completely separated from development.

Iterative / Incremental (Spiral, Unified Process)

All stages are performed in repeated iterations. Software is built and tested in increments.

Agile (Scrum, Kanban, XP, FDD)

Both static and dynamic at all levels. Lightweight documentation. Extensive automation. Experience-based manual testing.

Exam tip: Good testing practices apply to all SDLC models: Each development activity has a corresponding testing activity. Each test level has its own specific objectives.

⬅️ Shift-Left and Test-First Approaches

TDD

Tests are designed from the test basis before code. Code is written one test case at a time, tested, and refactored.

ATDD

Tests are derived from acceptance criteria during the design phase. Collaboration: developers + testers + business.

BDD

Test cases based on expected behavior in natural language (Gherkin format).

Given [context]
When [action]
Then [expected outcome]
Exam tip: Shift-left means testing happens earlier in the SDLC. Examples: review specifications from a tester's perspective, write test automation BEFORE code, use CI/CD, static analysis.

📝 The Review Process — 5 Steps

STEP 1
Planning
Define scope (purpose, material, quality characteristics), exit criteria, select participants, plan schedule and effort, determine which parts to review.
STEP 2
Review Initiation
Distribute material to reviewers. Inform participants of objectives and work process. Answer participant questions.
STEP 3
Individual Review
Each reviewer examines the work product individually. Logs potential defects, other observations, and questions. Done independently and carefully by each reviewer.
STEP 4
Communication and Analysis
Communicate potential defects (e.g. in a review meeting). Analyze defects, define owner and status. Evaluate and document quality characteristics. Decide review outcome: accepted / accepted with minor changes / rejected. Create meeting minutes (for formal types).
STEP 5
Fixing and Reporting
Create defect reports. Fix defects (done by the author). Follow up that exit criteria are met. Report review results (collect metrics, especially for formal reviews).

👥 Review Roles

Author Creator of the material being reviewed. Fixes defects found.
Manager Responsible for review planning. Assigns staff, budget, time.
Review Leader Overall responsibility for the review. Determines participants, time, and location.
Moderator (Facilitator) Ensures effective execution of review meetings. Moderates discussions.
Reviewer Reviews the material. Notes defects, findings, questions.
Scribe (Recorder) Collects defects. Takes minutes during the review meeting.
Exam tip: One person can hold multiple roles (especially in small organizations) — except in an inspection where the author has clearly restricted roles.

🔲 Equivalence Partitioning (EP)

Core Principles

  • Values are divided into classes where each member is treated equally
  • Testing with one value from a class is sufficient
  • A value can only belong to one equivalence class
  • Classes can be valid or invalid
  • Invalid classes must be tested individually (to avoid masking)
Exam tip: 100% EP coverage = all identified partitions (incl. invalid) covered at least once. "Each Choice coverage" — simplest criterion for multiple sets of partitions.

Example — Medicine Storage

Below +5°C: INVALID (medicine damaged)
+5°C to +25°C: VALID (medicine usable)
Above +25°C: INVALID (medicine damaged)
Test values:
-2°C (invalid class 1)
+10°C (valid class)
+38°C (invalid class 2)

📏 Boundary Value Analysis (BVA)

Core Principles

Defects cluster at boundaries between equivalence classes where program behavior changes. Requires ordered partitions (EP must be done first).

2-value technique

2 test cases per boundary: the boundary value itself AND the nearest value in the neighboring class.

Integer, +5°C to +25°C:
Lower boundary: {4, 5}
Upper boundary: {25, 26}

3-value technique

3 test cases per boundary: the boundary value AND one value on each side. More thorough — finds defects the 2-value technique misses.

Integer, +5°C to +25°C:
Lower boundary: {4, 5, 6}
Upper boundary: {24, 25, 26}

Boundary between two valid classes — example

€100–199 (2.5% discount) | €200–499 (5% discount)
3-value: {198, 199, 200} AND {199, 200, 201}
→ minimized set: {198, 199, 200, 201}
Exam tip: 2-value technique = 2 items per boundary. 3-value technique = 3 items per boundary. 100% BVA coverage requires all boundary values and neighboring values to be covered.

📋 Decision Table Testing

Core Principles

  • Systematically handles situations where behavior is affected by multiple conditions simultaneously
  • Limited entry: Boolean (True/False, Yes/No)
  • Extended entry: Multiple values per condition
  • Number of combinations = product of possible values per condition
  • Can reveal gaps in specifications

Example — 3 conditions (2×2×2 = 8 columns)

C1C2C3C4C5C6C7C8
C1YYYYNNNN
C2YYNNYYNN
C3YNYNYNYN
A1X
Exam tip: Combinations grow exponentially — a risk-based approach is needed with many conditions. 100% coverage = all feasible combinations tested.

🔄 State Transition Testing

Components

  • State: A condition the system can be in
  • Transition: Movement from one state to another
  • Event: Triggers a transition
  • Guard condition: Condition that must be true for a specific transition
  • Action: What happens as a result of the event

Coverage types (weakest → strongest)

1
All states coverage (100%)All states visited at least once
2
Valid transitions coverage (0-switch, 100%)All valid transitions exercised. Most commonly used criterion. Guarantees 100% state coverage, but NOT vice versa.
3
All transitions coverage (100%)All valid + attempts at all invalid transitions. Recommended minimum for safety/mission-critical software.
Exam tip: State table (rows = states, columns = events) is easier for finding invalid transitions than a diagram alone.

⚪ White-box Techniques

Statement Coverage

Exercises all executable statements in the program to the desired coverage level.

Statement coverage = (exercised statements / all executable statements) × 100%
  • Goal: usually 100%
  • Does NOT necessarily find data-dependent defects
  • Weakest of the code coverage types

Branch Coverage

Exercises all branches (control transfers between nodes) in the program.

Branch coverage = (exercised branches / all branches) × 100%
  • 100% branch coverage guarantees 100% statement coverage — but NOT vice versa
  • Conditional branches: true/false at decision points
  • Unconditional branches: straight-line code
Exam tip: Key relationship: 100% branch → 100% statement, but 100% statement ≠ 100% branch. Use white-box to see what was actually exercised — impossible with black-box alone.

🔮 Experience-based Techniques

Error Guessing

Tester uses knowledge and experience to "guess" where defects might occur.

Input errorsOutput formatData typeLogic errors

Fault attacks: systematic list of errors → test cases designed against these lists

Exploratory Testing

Simultaneous test design, execution, logging, and learning to understand the software better.

Session-based test management: time-boxed sessions with a test charter, followed by a debrief with stakeholders.

Checklist-based Testing

Uses checklists to focus testing on specific types of situations.

  • Write items in question format
  • Non-automatable items
  • Review regularly
  • More coverage, less repeatability

🤝 Collaborative Approaches — User Stories and ATDD

INVEST criteria for user stories

  • Independent — can be developed and estimated separately
  • Negotiable — details refined in conversation
  • Valuable — provides value to customer/business
  • Estimateable — team can estimate effort
  • Small — usually a few person-weeks
  • Testable — a test can be written for it

ATDD — 4 Steps

1
Analyze, Discuss, Fix
Specification workshop — developers, testers, business analyze user story together
2
Create examples and distill
Test cases = examples of expected behavior. Start with positive, add negative and non-functional.
3
Automate tests
Test cases are automated and integrated with the CI process
4
Implement functionality
Implement code that satisfies acceptance criteria; run tests until they pass

📋 Test Plan — Content and Planning

Test plan content (ISO/IEC/IEEE 29119-3)

  • Test context (scope, objectives, test basis)
  • Assumptions and constraints
  • Stakeholders (roles, responsibilities)
  • Communication requirements
  • Risk register (product and project risks)
  • Test approach (levels, types, techniques, deliverables)
  • Entry and exit criteria
  • Budget and schedule

Planning levels (iterative development)

Release planning

Entire release (months ahead). High-level. Product backlog, test approach for all iterations.

Iteration planning

Single sprint. Detailed. User story selection, risk analysis, effort estimation, test task decomposition.

🚪 Entry and Exit Criteria

Entry Criteria ("Definition of Ready")Exit Criteria ("Definition of Done")
PurposeEnsure conditions exist BEFORE testing beginsDefine WHEN testing is complete
Typical elementsResources (people, tools, environment, data, budget), testware, quality level of test objectCoverage metrics, defect density, number of failed test cases, binary yes/no criteria, schedule/budget thresholds
RelationshipExit criteria for one level can serve as entry criteria for the next level. Risks are communicated and accepted when stopping without meeting exit criteria.

⚠️ Risk Management

Risk Level = Likelihood × Impact

Risk = a potential future problem. If it has already occurred, it is a problem (issue).

Project Risk

Related to project management and control. CANNOT be affected by testing.

  • Organizational: delays, wrong estimates, budget cuts
  • People: missing skills, conflicts, communication problems
  • Technical: scope creep, unclear requirements, poor tool support
  • Supplier: third-party failures, contract problems

Product Risk

Related to quality characteristics. CAN be affected by testing.

  • Software does not perform defined functions
  • Wrong calculations, runtime errors
  • Poor architecture, inefficient algorithms
  • Problems with non-functional requirements
  • Consequences: user dissatisfaction, loss of revenue/reputation, legal penalties

📐 Estimation Techniques

Metrics-based techniques

  • Ratio-based: Based on data from previous projects. E.g.: if testing = 50% of development, and development = 600 days → testing = 300 days
  • Extrapolation: Uses actual project progress to estimate remaining work. Suitable for iterative projects.

Expert-based techniques

  • Wideband Delphi: Group of experts → individual estimates → presentation → discussion → repeated rounds until consensus. Agile variant: Planning Poker
  • Three-point estimation: E = (a + 4×m + b) / 6 · SD = (b – a) / 6 · (a=optimistic, m=most likely, b=pessimistic)

⚡ Benefits of Test Automation

Reduction of repetitive manual work

Regression tests, entering the same test data, compliance checking

Better consistency and repeatability

The tool executes tests identically every time — eliminates human error in execution

More objective assessment

Coverage measurements, test metrics

Easier access to information

Statistics on progress, defects, execution duration

Reduced test execution time

Faster defect detection, faster feedback, faster time-to-market

More time for testers

Can focus on creative/critical tests that tools cannot perform

⚠️ Risks of Test Automation

Unrealistic expectations

The tool may not solve the intended problem. May not be as easy as promised.

Underestimated effort

Training, integration, maintenance. Version upgrades require ongoing resources.

Using automation when manual is more effective

Not all tests should be automated.

Over-reliance on tools

Tools don't think. They don't find errors in the test basis. They cannot replace testers.

Vendor problems

Vendor may cease, stop support, or be sold to another vendor.

Open-source risks

External development may stop. The company must be able to continue development itself.

Platform/technology incompatibility

New technology may not be supported by existing tools.

Lack of compliance

Tools may not meet regulatory requirements or standards.

Key Principle

Tools are not a substitute for people. Testers cannot be removed from software development projects because of automation.

Exam tip: Questions about automation often focus on risks and limitations. Automation cannot replace human thinking, creativity, or understanding of business value.
